RRK 2.0 – Recursion & System Self‑Reference

How systems generate themselves without external control

Abstract

RRK 2.0 defines recursive systems as structures that generate their own states through internal operators. A system is not merely “affected” by rules; it produces its own dynamics through repeated application of a recursion operator. RRK 2.0 thus forms the first explicit formalization of system self‑reference within the RRK framework.

1. Motivation

Classical systems theory separates system and environment, rules and states. RRK 2.0 starts from a different premise:

A system is what recursively generates itself.

Instead of treating external control or input as the primary explanation, RRK 2.0 places internal recursion logic at the center.

2. Definition

A recursive system consists of:

\[ S_t \quad \text{(state at time } t) \]

\[ R \quad \text{(recursion operator)} \]

The system dynamics are given by:

\[ S_{t+1} = R(S_t) \]

The system generates its future states by repeatedly applying the same operator to its own states.

3. Formal Structure

(1) State space: \( S_t \in \mathcal{S} \)

(2) Operator: \( R: \mathcal{S} \rightarrow \mathcal{S} \)

(3) Recursion chain:

\[ S_0 \xrightarrow{R} S_1 \xrightarrow{R} S_2 \xrightarrow{R} \dots \]

(4) Self‑reference: The system acts upon itself; it is not only an object but also the source of its own dynamics.

4. Examples

Biology: Gene regulation recursively generates cell states.

Social systems: Communication generates further communication.

AI: Recursive activations in neural networks.

Mathematics: Sequences \( x_{n+1} = f(x_n) \).

5. Consequences

Systems are actively self‑generating, not passively reactive.

Stability emerges from the form of \( R \) and the structure of \( \mathcal{S} \).

Recursion produces drift, variation, and attractors in the state space.

RRK 2.0 defines recursive systems and their self‑reference. RRK 2.1 extends this perspective with drift and stabilization: How do recursive systems change over time, and under what conditions do stable patterns or attractors emerge?
