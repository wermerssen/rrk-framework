RRK 6.1 – Axiomatic Framework of Recursive Systems

The fully formal version of the RRK framework

Executive Summary

RRK 6.1 is the first fully axiomatic version of the RRK framework. While RRK 4.4 describes political transformation structurally and RRK 5.0 identifies the universal meta‑structures of recursive systems, RRK 6.1 formalizes these insights into a compact system of axioms. Recursive systems are described as combinations of state spaces, transformation operators, distance measures, recursion relations, and coherence/decoherence mechanisms. RRK 6.1 is therefore no longer a model of a specific system, but a general, domain‑independent structural framework for recursion.

Abstract

RRK 6.1 defines recursive systems through a set of axioms that formally capture states, transformations, recursion relations, coherence, decoherence, and attractor structures. Building on RRK 4.4 (political transformation) and RRK 5.0 (meta‑structures of recursive systems), RRK 6.1 describes how recursive dynamics can be modeled as formal systems independent of domain. The framework is constructed to represent both concrete applications (e.g., political, technological, biological systems) and meta‑analyses of recursive theory development.

1. Position within the RRK Framework

RRK 6.1 stands at the end of a recursive theory‑development process:

2.0 → 3.0 → 4.0 → 4.4 → 5.0 → 6.0 → 6.1

RRK 4.4 describes political transformation as a recursive structural model. RRK 5.0 abstracts this structure into universal meta‑properties of recursive systems. RRK 6.1 consolidates these insights into an axiomatic system that makes recursive systems formally describable independent of domain or content.

2. Basic Structure of Recursive Systems in RRK 6.1

RRK 6.1 describes a recursive system as the tuple

\[ \mathcal{R} = (Z, F, d, \varepsilon_r, R^\*, C_r, D_r, \rho, \sim) \]

with the following components:

  • \(Z\): state space of the system.
  • \(F\): set of transformation operators \(f : Z \to Z\).
  • \(d\): distance or divergence measure on \(Z\).
  • \(\varepsilon_r\): recursion threshold (tolerance for structural change).
  • \(R^\*\): recursion relation (recursive generation of state sequences).
  • \(C_r\): coherence relation (structure preservation).
  • \(D_r\): decoherence relation (structural dissolution).
  • \(\rho\): attractor or stability operator.
  • \(\sim\): equivalence or similarity relation on states or structures.

3. Axiom System of RRK 6.1

RRK 6.1 defines recursive systems not through content, but through axioms that determine the structure of their dynamics.

3.1 Recursion Axiom

There exists a recursion relation \(R^\*\) such that for at least one initial state \(z_0 \in Z\) a sequence \((z_n)\) is defined with

\[ z_{n+1} = f_n(z_n), \quad f_n \in F \]

and \(R^\*(z_0, z_n)\) holds for all \(n\).

3.2 Threshold Axiom

There exists a recursion threshold \(\varepsilon_r > 0\) such that structural transformations count as phase transitions when

\[ d(z_n, z_{n+1}) \ge \varepsilon_r \]

and these exceedances are not compensated by short‑term return to an equivalent state.

3.3 Coherence Axiom

A coherence relation \(C_r\) is defined such that for a sequence \((z_n)\):

\[ C_r(z_n, z_{n+1}) \Rightarrow d(z_n, z_{n+1}) < \varepsilon_r \]

Coherence means: recursion changes the state without leaving the structural class.

3.4 Decoherence Axiom

A decoherence relation \(D_r\) is defined such that:

\[ D_r(z_n, z_{n+1}) \Rightarrow d(z_n, z_{n+1}) \ge \varepsilon_r \]

Decoherence means: recursion leads to structural reorganization and potentially to new attractors.

3.5 Attractor Axiom

There exists an operator \(\rho\) that defines an attractor \(\rho(S)\) for suitable subsets \(S \subseteq Z\), such that for sufficiently large \(n\):

\[ z_n \in \rho(S) \quad \text{or} \quad d(z_n, \rho(S)) < \varepsilon_r. \]

3.6 Equivalence Axiom

A relation \(\sim\) is defined that describes structural equivalence or functional similarity between states or attractor structures. It is at least reflexive and symmetric, and in many applications also transitive.

4. Connection to RRK 4.4 and RRK 5.0

RRK 6.1 is not detached from earlier versions, but formalizes them:

  • RRK 4.4 can be interpreted as a special case of \(\mathcal{R}\), where \(Z\) represents political system states, \(F\) political transformation mechanisms, and \(\varepsilon_r\) political thresholds.
  • RRK 5.0 describes hyper‑recursion, meta‑thresholds, and meta‑feedbacks; RRK 6.1 provides the formal level on which such meta‑structures can be modeled as operations on \(\mathcal{R}\) itself.

5. Application Framework

RRK 6.1 is domain‑neutral. It can be applied to political, technological, biological, economic, or epistemic systems, as long as they can be modeled as recursive systems with states, transformations, and thresholds.

The concrete interpretation of \(Z, F, d, \varepsilon_r, R^\*, C_r, D_r, \rho, \sim\) depends on the application, but the structural logic remains invariant.

6. RRK as a Recursive Scientific Artifact

RRK 6.1 closes the recursive loop of its own theory‑genesis: the framework not only describes recursive systems, but can itself be reconstructed as a recursive system with versioning, variation, selection, and retention. The axiomatization in 6.1 makes this meta‑character explicit.

7. Versioning and Development

RRK has a documented version history:

2.0 → 3.0 → 4.0 → 4.4 → 5.0 → 6.0 → 6.1

RRK 6.1 is the first fully axiomatic version of the model. It consolidates the structural and meta‑structural insights of previous versions into a compact, universally applicable framework of recursive systems.

8. Conclusion

RRK 6.1 marks the transition from a recursive theory framework to a formal axiomatic system of recursive systems. It connects the politically motivated structural perspective of RRK 4.4 with the meta‑level of RRK 5.0 and transforms both into a domain‑independent, precisely defined structure. RRK thus becomes a general tool for formally analyzing recursion, thresholds, coherence, decoherence, and attractor formation in complex systems.
