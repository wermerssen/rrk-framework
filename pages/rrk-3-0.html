RRK 3.0 – Global Theory

Global attractor geometry and systemic transitions

Overview

RRK 3.0 extends the recursive–relational framework to a global dynamical theory. It describes how entire attractor landscapes reorganize under parameter changes, and how systemic transitions emerge from global rather than local mechanisms.

Formally, RRK 3.0 considers a family of dynamical systems \( F_\lambda : X \to X \) with parameter \( \lambda \in \Lambda \), and studies the structure of attractors and their basins across \(\Lambda\).

Global attractor geometry

RRK 3.0 introduces the concept of global attractor geometry: the structure formed by all attractors \(A_i(\lambda)\), their basins \(B_i(\lambda)\), and the boundaries between them.

A minimal representation of the global attractor geometry is given by

\[ \mathcal{G}(\lambda) = \big\{ (A_i(\lambda), B_i(\lambda)) \big\}_{i=1}^N, \]

together with the adjacency relations between basins and their separating manifolds. RRK 3.0 treats this structure as the primary object of analysis, rather than individual trajectories.

Transition manifolds

A central innovation of RRK 3.0 is the notion of transition manifolds: geometric surfaces in parameter space that separate qualitatively distinct global regimes.

Informally, a transition manifold \(\mathcal{T}\) is a subset of parameter space such that small perturbations across \(\mathcal{T}\) induce a reconfiguration of the global attractor geometry:

\[ \lambda \in \mathcal{T} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathcal{G}(\lambda^-)\not\simeq \mathcal{G}(\lambda^+), \]

where \(\lambda^-\) and \(\lambda^+\) denote parameters on opposite sides of \(\mathcal{T}\). Crossing such a manifold corresponds to a systemic transition.

Global stability balance

Instead of local eigenvalues, RRK 3.0 defines stability in terms of the global stability balance: the relative strength of attractors and the geometry of their basins.

Let \(A_i\) denote attractors with basin measures \(\mu(B_i)\) and stability weights \(w_i\). The global stability balance is defined as

\[ S_{\text{global}}(\lambda) = \frac{\sum_{i} w_i(\lambda)\,\mu\big(B_i(\lambda)\big)} {\sum_{i} \mu\big(B_i(\lambda)\big)}. \]

Changes in \(S_{\text{global}}\) across parameter space indicate shifts in the dominance of attractors and thus in the systemic regime.
